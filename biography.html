The Life of April Greiman

April Greiman was born in New York City in 1948 and grew up surrounded by creativity, culture, and visual communication. However, her formal design training occurred overseas at the Basel School of Design in Switzerland, where she studied under the influential designer Wolfgang Weingart.

Weingart is often credited as the father of New Wave typography, and Greiman became one of his most notable students during the 1970s. His experimental approach would shape the rest of her career.

"In Basel, I learned that design could be both systematic and poetic." — April Greiman on her education

In Basel, Greiman learned to respect modernist principles while also pushing their boundaries. Traditional Swiss design emphasized clarity, grid systems, and strict typographic rules. But her mentor encouraged students to stretch, distort, rotate, and challenge these conventions.

Greiman carried this mindset with her when she returned to the United States, bringing a fresh creative philosophy that American design had not yet seen.

Swiss Foundation

At the Basel School of Design, Greiman absorbed the International Typographic Style but soon began questioning its limitations. Under Weingart's guidance, she explored:

  • Experimental typographic arrangements
  • Layering of photographic elements
  • Color theory beyond corporate palettes
  • The expressive potential of grid breaking

California Transformation

In 1976, she moved to Los Angeles, a transition that deeply influenced her artistic identity. The California design scene was less rigid and more experimental than the East Coast.

Surrounded by film, technology, and the vibrant colors of the West Coast environment, Greiman began blending European training with American cultural energy. This led to her signature style: expressive, layered, colorful, and technologically innovative.

Greiman's studio, Made in Space, became a hub for digital experimentation

Greiman founded her studio, Made in Space, in 1979. From this studio, she produced a wide range of projects including graphic identities, posters, magazine layouts, environmental graphics, and digital design compositions.

Digital Pioneer

Her work often incorporated photography, video imagery, and early computer-generated elements. One of her most famous achievements is the 1986 issue of Design Quarterly, in which she created an enormous 6-foot fold-out poster assembled entirely from a Macintosh computer.

This piece is considered one of the earliest major examples of digital graphic design and marked a turning point in how designers approached technology.

Teaching Philosophy

As her career developed, Greiman became a key figure in design education as well. She taught at various universities including:

California Institute of the Arts

Encouraged interdisciplinary approaches

ArtCenter College of Design

Integrated technology into curriculum

Woodbury University

Established digital design programs

Her philosophy emphasized playful curiosity, experimentation, and embracing the imperfections of digital tools. She encouraged designers to explore emerging technologies rather than fear them.
